Favorite Books

A running list of books that shaped how I think.
Click any title to flip it.
Billy SummersStephen King

King at his most human. A hitman who pretends to be dumb reads Steinbeck between kills — it's a thriller but really about storytelling and what we hide from the world. One of his best.

The Dutch HouseAnn Patchett

Siblings who can't stop orbiting the house that shaped them. The prose is so clean it almost hurts — I kept reading sentences twice.

Beneath a Scarlet SkyMark Sullivan

Based on the true story of a teenage boy smuggling Jews over the Alps in WWII. Heart-stopping and completely unforgettable.

When Breath Becomes AirPaul Kalanithi

A neurosurgeon diagnosed with terminal cancer writes about what makes life worth living. I've recommended this to every person I know.

The Spy and the TraitorBen Macintyre

The true story of the most valuable spy of the Cold War. Reads like a thriller but every word is real. Ben Macintyre is the best nonfiction writer alive.

Tomorrow, and Tomorrow, and TomorrowGabrielle Zevin

About creativity, collaboration, and two people who love each other but can never quite figure out how. The video game world is just a backdrop for the most honest love story I've read.

Cutting for StoneAbraham Verghese

Twin brothers born in Ethiopia, separated by fate. Medicine, love, identity, and war braided across continents and decades. Completely immersive.

BabelR.F. Kuang

Dark academia about translation, colonialism, and empire. Oxford has never felt so sinister. Genuinely changed how I think about language.

The Book ThiefMarkus Zusak

Narrated by Death during WWII. Liesel stealing books in wartime Germany is one of the most beautiful stories ever written.

The Boy in the Striped PajamasJohn Boyne

Two nine-year-olds on opposite sides of a concentration camp fence. The innocence is what destroys you.

The Diary of a Young GirlAnne Frank

She was my age when she wrote this. That's all I keep thinking about. The humanity on every single page.

The MartianAndy Weir

The most fun I've ever had reading about surviving alone on Mars. Mark Watney's voice is irresistible. Science has never been this funny.

A Little LifeHanya Yanagihara

The most emotionally devastating book I've ever read. Four friends in New York, one carrying a trauma beyond imagination. You will cry. A lot.

CirceMadeline Miller

The witch of Greek mythology finally speaks for herself. A story about power, solitude, and finding your own magic.

PachinkoMin Jin Lee

Four generations of a Korean family in Japan across most of the twentieth century. Epic doesn't begin to cover it.

Demon CopperheadBarbara Kingsolver

David Copperfield transplanted into Appalachia during the opioid crisis. Raw, real, and Pulitzer-winning for a reason.

HorseGeraldine Brooks

A lost painting, a skeleton, and a legendary racehorse connect people across centuries. Brooks makes history feel urgent.

The Song of AchillesMadeline Miller

Achilles and Patroclus retold as the love story it always was. I finished it on a plane and sat very still for a while afterward.

Harry PotterJ.K. Rowling

My whole childhood. The books that made me a reader. I find something new every time I reread them.

On WritingStephen King

Half memoir, half masterclass. The best book about writing I've ever read — and I'm not a writer. His voice is so alive on every page.

The Catcher in the RyeJ.D. Salinger

I actually loved this. Holden's anger felt so honest, and the loneliness underneath all that bravado hit close to home.

Into Thin AirJon Krakauer

Krakauer was on the 1996 Everest expedition that killed eight people and wrote about it immediately after. The best and most haunting adventure writing I've ever read.

The Secret HistoryDonna Tartt

A group of classics students at a Vermont college commit a murder — and you know this from page one. The whole book is the slow unraveling of how and why. Dark academia at its absolute best.
